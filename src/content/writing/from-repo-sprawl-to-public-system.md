A large GitHub account can look messy from the outside.

That does not always mean the work is weak. Sometimes it means the work has not been curated.

The difference matters.

## Sprawl Hides Patterns

When every repository is presented equally, visitors cannot see the main direction.

Old prototypes, package experiments, forks, archived research artifacts, and current flagship work all compete for attention.

That makes serious work harder to notice.

## Curation Reveals The System

Curation means organizing repositories by role:

- flagship work;
- supporting packages;
- legacy predecessors;
- prototypes;
- research archives;
- archived noise;
- private or controlled material.

This does not erase history. It gives history a shape.

## The Strong Line

The strongest public line in my work is now clear:

- Laravel/PHP backend engineering;
- Nexus Scholar;
- systematic review tooling;
- scholarly providers;
- citation graphs;
- reference management;
- guarded plant-disease AI research.

That story was already present in the repositories. It just needed to be made visible.

## Not Every Repo Needs Promotion

Some repos should remain public but unpromoted. Some should be archived. Some should be labeled as predecessors. Some should only be referenced when a specific article needs them.

The goal is not to make every old repo look important.

The goal is to make the current public direction obvious.

## Evidence Beats Apology

A public identity should not apologize for old work.

It should show:

- what matters now;
- what supports the current direction;
- what is legacy;
- what is intentionally controlled;
- what evidence backs the claims.

That is why this site exists. It turns scattered public work into a navigable system.

Plant-disease AI is part of my research direction.

It also needs a careful public boundary. The broad area can be public. The exact unpublished methods, future experiments, and sensitive thesis planning should not be public until they are ready.

That is the right balance for now.

## What Is Safe To Say

It is safe to say that the research lane involves:

- plant-disease AI;
- computer vision;
- tomato-related benchmark work;
- segmentation and visual disease analysis at a broad level;
- benchmark-safe evaluation;
- reproducible workflow discipline.

This communicates the domain without exposing fragile details.

## What Should Stay Private

It is not safe to publish everything.

I should avoid public details such as:

- exact unpublished thesis methods;
- future experiment sequence;
- weak-label strategy before results are ready;
- pseudo-label strategy before results are ready;
- unsupported field-deployment claims;
- treatment recommendation claims;
- yield improvement claims;
- claims of broad state of the art without final evidence.

Those boundaries protect the research.

## Public Evidence Can Still Exist

Careful boundaries do not mean silence.

Public evidence can include:

- repository descriptions;
- workflow archives;
- benchmark-safe notes;
- dataset provenance boundaries;
- scripts or documentation that do not expose sensitive strategy;
- final results once they are ready.

The public goal is to show research discipline, not to leak the thesis plan.

## Why This Belongs On The Site

The site connects software and research.

Nexus Scholar shows research workflow tooling. The plant-disease AI lane shows the scientific context that motivates careful evidence handling. Both reinforce the same public identity: backend systems and research software where auditability matters.

## Current Rule

For now, plant-disease AI writing should stay broad, benchmark-safe, and provenance-aware.

That gives enough public signal without creating problems for unpublished work.
